Introduction

IDEA, full name as "Interactive Differential Expression Analyzer", is an online analysis and visualization platform for differential gene expression analysis of reads count data on foundation of R, Shiny and JavaScript. In IDEA, five R packages, DESeq, EdgeR, SAMSeq, PoissonSeq and NOISeq are provided for counts data analysis.

The function of this app is divided into "Data Exploration", "Analysis" and "Integrative Analysis" three modules. In "Data Exploration", the input data can be normalized, and several figures, such as PCA and Boxplot, are provided to reveal the characteristic of the data. These analyses can help to estimate the data quality. For "Analysis", the five packages are provided to estimate the differential expression of features. Certain tables and figures of each package can give a clear and visual result of the estimation. The last part "Integrative Analysis" synthesizes the results of each package and makes a comparison, providing an easier way to obtain the result of differential expression features.

This document is a step-by-step guidance and instruction of IDEA, and follows the order of operations.

Introduction to Normalization, Data Exploration and Quality Control

The "Data" module does the job of data normalization, data exploration and quality control.

The process of normalization attempts to settle the problem of various factors (nucleotide composition of genes, library preparation and sequencing platform etc.) which can bring bias into number of reads in RNA-Seq data. 3 methods are provided to normalize sample data (Table 2-1).

TMM Trimmed Mean of M Compute a scaling factor as weighted means of log ratios between two experiments after excluding most expressed and genes that have large log ratios in expression

Differential Expression Gene Analysis - "Analysis"

Introduction to Differential Expression Analysis

In IDEA, we provide five methods for analyzing differential expression of genes. Here we simply introduce the basic feature of every method. Class A are the packages using negative binomial distribution as models and Class B are the packages using nonparametric method. The summary table is given below (Table 3-1).

Class A: Negative Binomial Distribution

edgeR

edgeR adopts a quartile-adjusted conditional maximum likelihood estimator to determine overdispersion parameters in negative binomial distribution, which has improved performance than standard maximum likelihood estimator, especially when there are few replicates per condition. Dispersion parameters are later refined by adjusting towards a consensus value using cross-transcript information via empirical Bayes procedure. Differentially expressed genes are validated by an exact test to determine if read counts under different conditions fit the same negative binomial distribution.

DESeq

DESeq takes all transcripts with similar expression values into consideration to determine overdispersion parameters in different conditions, assuming that overdispersion is related to mean read count. Differentially expressed genes are validated by an exact test between means of distribution parameters in two conditions for equality. In IDEA, DESeq has last updated to DESeq2 (1.6.2).

Class B: Non Negative Binomial Distribution

NOISeq

NOISeq takes both the log fold change and absolute difference of read counts between two conditions into consideration. All pairs of replicates are calculated to determine empirical distribution functions. Differentially expressed genes are identified by a small P-value in both the log fold change and the absolute difference in read counts.

SAMseq

SAMSeq carries out Wilcoxon test between transcript counts under two different conditions, followed by subsampling for normalization. To obtain significant differential expression, higher number of samples per condition is required.

PoissonSeq

PoissonSeq transforms read counts to fit into Poisson log-linear model, and then calculates the significance of differential expression between different conditions using a score statistic on model parameters.

Interpreting Figures and Tables

Common Visualization

The figures and tables lay out below are available in all the packages.

MA-Plot

In MA-Plot, the data is been transformed onto the M (fold change or log ratio) and A (average expression of a gene) scale, which can give users a quick overview of the distribution of data. In IDEA, the log2–transformed fold change is plotted on the y-axis and the average count (normalized by size factors) is on the x-axis. The false discovery rate (FDR) threshold can be changed, and the genes are colored red if the adjusted p-value is less than the FDR, while other genes are colored blue.

Figure 3.3.1.1 MA-plot

Normalized Size Factors

Since different samples may have different sequencing depth, it is necessary to put every count value to a common scale in order to make them comparable. This work is done by estimating the relative size factors of each sample, then divide the samples by the size factors separately.

In the table, group represents conditions, lib.size represents size of the library, norm.factors is the normalized size factors. The number of rows of genes to view is changeable.

Table 3.3.1.2 Table of Normalized Size Factors

Volcano Plot

An overview of the number of differential expression genes can be shown in the volcano plot. Volcano plot is a scatter plot with log2-transformed fold change on the x-axis, and the y-axis represents the–log10-transformed p-value. The threshold of both axes can be changed. Highly differential expressed genes are colored blue, while others are in red.

Figure 3.3.1.3 Volcano Plot

Heatmap of Differential Expressed Genes

By using a color scale, heatmap can display the expression values of the genes, and every rectangle represents one gene – sample pair. By default, we display the 30 most highly expressed genes and his number is changeable. In this heatmap, features are arranged in columns (samples) and rows (genes) as in the original data matrix. The color represents the log2-transformed RPKM+1.

Figure 3.3.1.4 Heatmap of differential expression genes

FDR Distribution Plot

FDR distribution plot visualizes distribution of FDR or P-value in differential expression test provided in analysis packages using histogram plot. It uses FDR or P-value as x-axis and percentage of different groups of x value as y-axis, and colors significant and not significant groups differently. Additionally, NOISeq doesn't have FDR distribution plot.

Variance Estimation (DESeq)

The variance estimation is a very important step of quality assessment. Usually this estimation has 3 steps: estimating the gene dispersion value; using the value to fit a curve; giving a dispersion value to each gene from either estimation or fitted value.

The dispersion estimates plot is for checking the result of dispersion estimates adjustment. The gene-wise estimates are in black, the fitted estimates are in red, and the final estimates are in blue. The outliers of gene-wise estimates are marked with blue circles. The points lying on the bottom indicates they have a dispersion of practically zero or exactly zero. This plot has mean of normalized counts as x-axis and dispersion as y-axis.

Variance estimation (edgeR)

In edgeR, the qCML(quantile-adjusted conditional maximum likelihood) is used to estimate the dispersion of single factor experiment analysis, since it is suitable for the data with many small samples and a common dispersion. The tag-wise (gene-wise) negative binominal estimation is done by empirical Bayes and the common dispersion is estimated by conditional common likelihood.

The variance estimation plot has average log CPM (counts per million) as x-axis and biological coefficient variation as y-axis. The red dots represent the common dispersion and the black dots represent the tag-wise (gene-wise) dispersion.

Fit Plots (SAMSeq)

The fit plot, also called the SAM plot in SAMSeq, is a scatter plot with dots representing genes. In SAM, the Cox model score test of every gene is counted. The positive significant genes, which means the genes has higher expression correlates with higher risk, are in red, and negative significant genes are in green, while others are in black. The expected score is plotted on the x-axis and observed score is on the y-axis.
